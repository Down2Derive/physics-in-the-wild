Who AM I?

I’m Jonathan James Guevara Rodriguez, an undergraduate physics student passionate about exploring the universe’s biggest mysteries. My interests focus on experimental physics, astrophysics, and the elusive world of dark matter — the stuff we know is out there but haven’t fully uncovered.

Right now, I’m working with large astronomical datasets, tackling projects like star-galaxy classification using LSST data. I enjoy the challenge of finding structure in the noise and pulling meaningful insights from complex data.

As a first-generation college student and science communicator, I’m committed to making physics approachable. I believe in translating complex concepts into engaging stories and visualizations — bridging the gap between data, discovery, and public understanding, while contributing to experimental research on some of the universe’s most fundamental mysteries.

Projects, Research & Involvement

Here are some of the projects I’m currently working on and areas I’m passionate about:

  • Star-Galaxy Classification with LSST Data: Analyzing large-scale survey data to improve classification accuracy and explore the limits of automated object detection.
  • Data From the Void — Dark Matter Exploration: Investigating the unseen mass in the universe through research concepts focused on observational evidence and experimental detection methods.
  • Science Communication & Outreach: Writing, visualizing, and communicating complex physics topics for general audiences — because physics belongs in the wild, not just in labs and papers.
  • Interactive Data Visualizations (Coming Soon): Building visual tools and plots to better represent astronomical data.
  • Physics Tutoring: Providing one-on-one and group tutoring sessions for high school and college students in physics, math, and astronomy — helping others strengthen their foundations in STEM.
  • Physics Club Member: Actively participating in my university’s physics club, collaborating with peers on projects, outreach, and scientific discussions that foster community and growth in the field.
  • Professional Memberships: Active member of the Society of Physics Students (SPS), American Physical Society (APS), and American Astronomical Society (AAS) — staying connected with the broader physics and astronomy research communities.
